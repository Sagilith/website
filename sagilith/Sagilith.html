Otolith Thermal Marking

Otolith thermal marking is a clever, non-invasive technique used primarily in salmon hatcheries to permanently "Mark" fish with a unique internal bar code.

What is an Otolith?

An otolith (from Greek: "ear stone") is a small, hard, calcium-carbonate structure in the inner ear of bony fish. It helps the fish with balance and hearing. As the fish grows, the otolith grows too by adding microscopic layers of material daily — much like how a tree adds wood.

How Otolith Thermal Marking Works

Hatcheries create artificial marks on the otolith while the fish is still an embryo and/or alevin:

  • They briefly change the water temperature in the incubation tanks (typically a drop of 3–5°C for 24–48 hours, then back to normal).
  • This abrupt change in temperature disrupts the normal daily growth of the otolith, creating a visible dark ring (or band) when viewed under a microscope with transmitted light.
  • By repeating controlled temperature cycles with different durations or timing, hatcheries can create a unique pattern or "barcode" of dark and light rings.
  • This pattern acts as a permanent internal mark that identifies the Country, State, hatchery, brood year, release site, or even a specific group of fish.

About Michael Wunderlich

Hi, I’m Michael Wunderlich, founder and principal consultant of Sagilith, a private fisheries laboratory specializing in otolith thermal marking and precise fish aging.

I began my career in British Columbia, working with otoliths and aging structures from rainbow trout, kokanee, lake trout, walleye, and pike. In 2005, I moved to Alaska to conduct thermal marking research at one of the largest salmon hatcheries in the state. Over the years, I’ve worked with the Alaska Department of Fish and Game, the North Pacific Anadromous Fish Commission, National Marine Fisheries Service, University of Alaska Southeast, Japan, Russia, and most major Alaska salmon hatcheries.

In 2013, I moved to Seattle, Washington and founded Sagilith. I now run thermal marking programs for major utility companies including Seattle Public Utilities and Tacoma Power.
